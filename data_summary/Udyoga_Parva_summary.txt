Summary of Udyoga parva. This is the summary of main parva, Udyoga parva.

**Summary**

The text describes various conversations between characters from the Mahabharata, including Dhritarashtra, Sanjaya, Shalya, Indra, Agastya, Vidura, Vaishampayana, Krishna, Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, and others. The conversations revolve around topics such as war, peace, dharma (righteous conduct), the importance of listening to wise advice, and the consequences of following a deviant path.

The text also describes various events leading up to the war between the Pandavas and the Kauravas, including the preparations for battle, the deployment of armies, and the strategies employed by both sides. The conversations highlight the military strengths and weaknesses of both armies and the importance of dharma in guiding their actions.

**Key events and characters**

- Dhritarashtra asks Sanjaya to visit Upaplavya and show homage to Ajatashatru (Yudhishthira)
- Shalya greets Indra and asks him about the means for killing Nahusha
- Agastya appears before Indra and greets him, explaining how Nahusha was dislodged from heaven
- Dhritarashtra instructs Sanjaya to visit Upaplavya again and persuade the Pandavas to make peace
- Vaishampayana narrates a conversation between King Dhritarashtra and Sanatsujata about death and immortality
- The king of Hastinapura, Dhritarashtra, asks his charioteer Sanjaya to describe the army assembled for the Pandavas
- Duryodhana argues that the Kauravas have a strong army and that their enemies are not invincible
- Krishna meets Kunti, who advises him on how to deal with Duryodhana
- Yudhishthira asks his brothers to suggest a general for their army, and they nominate various candidates
- The Pandavas prepare for war by setting up camp in Kurukshetra
- King Duryodhana deploys his eleven akshouhinis (armies) and instructs his soldiers on how to prepare for battle
- Uluka is sent by Duryodhana to speak to Yudhishthira, reminding him of past injustices and urging him to fight
- Bhishma becomes the commander of the Kouravas and lists various rathas (warriors) on both sides, including their strengths and weaknesses

**Philosophical or Moral Ideas**

- The importance of respecting and honoring sages and brahmarshis
- The consequences of arguing with or disrespecting those who are wise and knowledgeable
- The idea that good fortune can lead to prosperity and success
- Humility destroys bad conduct, valour destroys adverse circumstances, forgiveness always destroys anger, and good conduct destroys evil omens
- One should not do to others what is injurious to oneself, anger should be conquered with lack of anger, wickedness should be conquered with goodness, miserliness should be conquered with generosity, falsehood should be conquered with truth, and forgiveness is the strength of those who possess qualities
- Confusion is death, and immortality is where there is no confusion; dharma (righteous living) is eternal, happiness and unhappiness are transient, life is eternal but its constituent elements are transient
- The importance of following one's dharma (duty) and artha (interest)
- A true warrior relies on their own valour, not the valour of others; boasting is a sign of weakness, not strength; a person who challenges enemies based on the valour of others is among the worst of men
- The consequences of pride, insolence, anger, and harshness will lead to disaster
- Destiny is supreme, and human endeavour amounts to nothing (Dhritarashtra); a man should not ascribe the fault of his calamities to time or destiny (Sanjaya)
- A person's actions can lead to eternal misery for others; women can be strong and determined in their pursuit of revenge
