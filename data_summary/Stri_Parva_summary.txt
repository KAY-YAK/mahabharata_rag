Summary of Stri parva. This is the summary of main parva, Stri parva.

**Summary**

Janamejaya asks Vaishampayana about the events after Duryodhana's death. Dhritarashtra is overcome with grief, but Sanjaya consoles him by saying sorrowing doesn't help. Vidura explains the path of intelligence and how one can escape the cycle of life. Krishna tells Dhritarashtra that he should not grieve over his sons' deaths because it was destined to happen.

Later, Janamejaya asks Vaishampayana about King Dhritarashtra's actions after Vyasa departed. The king is overcome with sorrow and sees his sons and grandsons dead on the battlefield. Gandhari laments piteously and speaks to Krishna about the destruction of the Kurus. She also sees Duryodhana lying dead on the ground.

Vasudeva tells Gandhari not to sorrow unnecessarily, criticizing her for honoring Duryodhana's evil deeds as virtuous. Dhritarashtra asks Yudhishthira about the number of soldiers killed in battle and is told that over 1 billion and 66 million soldiers were slain. The funeral rites are then performed for all those who died.

The text also describes a scene where the Pandavas and their relatives gather at the Ganga river to perform rituals for their loved ones who died in the war. Kunti speaks about her eldest son Karna, telling the Pandavas that they should have considered him their brother and performed rituals for him.

**Key events and characters**

- Dhritarashtra is overcome with grief upon hearing about his sons' deaths.
- Sanjaya consoles Dhritarashtra by saying sorrowing doesn't help.
- Vidura explains the path of intelligence to Dhritarashtra.
- Krishna tells Dhritarashtra that he should not grieve over his sons' deaths because it was destined to happen.
- Gandhari laments piteously and speaks to Krishna about the destruction of the Kurus.
- Vasudeva tells Gandhari not to sorrow unnecessarily.
- Dhritarashtra asks Yudhishthira about the number of soldiers killed in battle.
- The funeral rites are performed for all those who died.
- Kunti speaks about her eldest son Karna and tells the Pandavas to consider him their brother.

**Philosophical or Moral Ideas**

- Sorrowing does not help, but rather one should perform funeral rites for those who have died.
- Death is the end of life, but it also leads to a higher state.
- Beings come into existence and cease to be due to their own deeds.
- One should not sorrow over something that affects the entire country and not a single person alone.
- The cycle of life is like a forest where beings are afflicted by diseases, old age, and other difficulties.
- One who is learned can escape from this cycle by controlling their senses and desires.
- Destiny cannot be crossed, and no being can deviate from a path that destiny has laid down.
- Excessive grief causes a double calamity.
- Actions during life determine one's fate after death.
- Knowledge and understanding can help individuals navigate difficult situations.
- The importance of considering all members of a family as one's own, regardless of their birth or social status.
