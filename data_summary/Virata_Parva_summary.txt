Summary of Virata parva. This is the summary of main parva, Virata parva.

**Summary**

The story revolves around the Pandavas, who are forced to live in disguise after being exiled from their kingdom. They take on different roles in the city of Matsya (Virata) and live there for a year while maintaining their true identities hidden.

Droupadi lives in disguise as Sairandhri and faces unhappiness due to her situation. She expresses her grief to Bhima, who promises to kill Kichaka, Virata's general who has been molesting her. Droupadi fixes an assignation with Kichaka, where Bhima kills him in secret.

Karna is advised by Kripa not to fight Arjuna alone due to his many skills and accomplishments. However, Ashvatthama criticizes Karna for boasting about his past victories without actually performing any great deeds. The Kurus arrange themselves in battle formations, but Arjuna swiftly advances towards them and defeats many warriors.

King Virata of Matsya forms an alliance with the Pandavas during their exile and welcomes them back when they reveal themselves. An alliance is formed between the Matsyas and the Pandavas, and their marriage takes place with great festivities.

**Philosophical or Moral Ideas**

- The importance of protecting one's wife and adhering to dharma (righteous living).
- The consequences of addiction and the loss of self-control.
- The power of destiny and the inevitability of fate.
- The need for women to be protected and respected in society.
- A war is not desirable if it's conducted at the wrong time or place (stated by Kripa).
- Valour should be used at the right time for one's welfare (stated by Kripa).
- It's foolish to fight a skilled and strong opponent alone (stated by Kripa).
- Unity and cooperation are necessary in battle (stated by Bhishma).
- The importance of forgiveness and seeking pardon for past mistakes.
- The value of self-control and restraint in speech and action.
- The concept of dharma (duty) and its importance in maintaining social order.
- The idea that true greatness lies not in wealth or power, but in character and virtue.
